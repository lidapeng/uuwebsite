Biography

    I was born in Weihai, a small city on the east coast of Shandong Province, China. I went to Beijing to attend college in 2004 and lived there for seven years. I spent four years in college and earned my Bachelor's degree from China University of Geosciences (Beijing), a geology school that has cultivated a great many geologists in China. As an undergraduate, I majored in Geographic Information Systems (GIS) in the School of Information Engineering and was trained as a software engineer via the four-year undergraduate program. After college graduation, I enrolled in the Masters program of Cartography and Geographic Information Systems at the Institute of Remote Sensing and GIS, Peking University. After I got my Masters degree,  I came to Utah to pursue my PhD in the Department of Geography at the University of Utah in August, 2011.

    My undergraduate study mainly focused on software engineering. With my engineering background, I worked as a research assistant for Dr. Chengqi Cheng at the Institute of Remote Sensing and GIS at Peking University.  I started to conduct research on Location-Based Services (LBS) and developed interest in this field. In 2008, an earthquake hit Sichuan Province, China and caused many deaths. Because I was shocked by first responders' unpreparedness for that earthquake, I started to pay attention to geospatial technologies in emergency management. I believe that geospatial technologies can make a difference if we make better use of them in disasters.

    At the University of Utah, I worked with Dr. Tom Cova (primary adviser) and Phil Dennison (co-adviser) on an National Science Foundation (NSF) project named "protective action triggers" (2011-2014), which aims to improve our understanding of evacuation timing and protectve action selection in wildfire evacuations. My dissertation focuses on wildfire evacuation modeling, which involves using fire spread modeling, traffic simulation, and GIS to model wildfire evacuation as a coupled human-environment system.

    During the fourth year (2014-2015), I worked as a research assistant for Dr. Neng Wan. My research focused on using smartphone accelerometers to study physical activity. My previous experience in mobile computing concentrated on mobile development and information systems, and this new field helped me sharpen my skills in mobile computing and develop a good understanding of its applications in public health. 

    In this fall, I am working as a research assistant for Dr. Quynh Nguyen on an NIH project that aims to use social media in public health applications. My responsibilities include spatial data processing and geovisualization. At the same time, I am also working on the last manuscript in my dissertation and trying to finish up my dissertation during the fall semester.
